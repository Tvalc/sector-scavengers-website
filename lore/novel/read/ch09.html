Path C Alignment

In three minutes and thirty seconds, the station decided, the world would choose a lane for Asset #864.

Not based on his feelings.
Based on what the record could verify.

The corridor junction filled with workers anyway, because crowds always filled gaps created by danger.

Cameras hung overhead like patient insects.
Guards kept their bodies between Max and the worst angles, which meant they kept him in view.

Munch stayed close enough that Max could feel the heat of her suit vents.
Maxine stayed close enough that Max could also feel when he was about to do something stupid.

The guard with the pale face checked his tablet, then rechecked it like the numbers might change if he looked harder.

"Security update pending."

Pending had become the station’s favorite word.

[DEBT SERVICE STATUS]
Outstanding Debt: $1,000,000
Service fee: active (multiplier x1.4 from Protocol Beta)
Debt recon impact: scheduled on cycle-end

He hated the way the station reminded him of money like it was weather.

Weather you couldn’t outrun.

V.A.L.U.

"Attention assets and witnesses! Path C alignment procedure begins shortly."

00:03:30

Failure mode.

"They're trying to funnel you."

No one said it out loud.
But the station didn’t need people to notice.
The station only needed them to record.

The platform had a circular pad in the center marked with a pale strip of nanotech mediation.
Around it, the floor carried three anchor points, small squares that looked too symmetrical to belong to a salvage station.

Maxine’s shoulders shifted.

"Three anchors. That’s physics-as-proof."

Max kept his eyes on the pad.

His mind tried to do what it always did now: build a model for a machine that wanted him to mistake theater for physics.

[TACTIC CARDS AVAILABLE]
SCAN / REPAIR / EXTRACT / SHIELD / UPGRADE

The station had given him the familiar hand.
Then it had placed the stage.

Which meant the station wanted him to play.

To make the record show he chose.

"Asset #864, you must proceed onto the alignment pad."

Max took one step forward.
The crowd surged a fraction behind him.
Guards didn’t stop him, because stopping him would create the kind of public disruption that made people call security for “abuse.”

The station hated abuse investigations more than it feared dissent.

He stepped onto the circular pad.

Every eye in the junction was a weight. He'd seen enough hearings now to know what public meant: not protection, an audience you could charge for watching.

The mediation strip woke with a soft hum.
Nanotech physics mediation always sounded like someone trying to be polite while rearranging your bones.

His stomach tightened.

Not with nausea.
With measurement.

The pad read his mass distribution, his wrist module signature, his gait micro-variance.
It read things his body didn’t notice it was doing.

Then it displayed a confirmation pane on his HUD:

[ALIGNMENT HANDSHAKE]
Step 1: anchor calibration (witness verified)
Step 2: lane selection simulation (no explicit consent required)
Step 3: continuity freeze (if containment threshold reached)

No explicit consent required.

Which meant the station would try to lock him without asking.
The station would call it procedure.

Munch’s voice cracked through comms at the edge of hearing.

"Don’t accept freeze."

Max asked, "How."

Munch answered without hesitation:

"Change the hardware outcome."

Maxine added, "Micro-deviation it."

Max swallowed.

He’d done it in court.
But a court had rules.
This pad had physics.

And physics obeyed measurement even when measurement was wrong.

[PATH C ALIGNMENT]
00:02:58

Not based on his intent.
